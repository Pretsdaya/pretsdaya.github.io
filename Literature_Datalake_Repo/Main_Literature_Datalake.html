VHCF Topics

VHCF testing presents a novel method for the assessment of the durability of materials subject to fatigue loading typically greater than 107 cycles at lower stress ranges than the low-and high cycle regime for most metallic materials.

Microstructure - Measuring Techniques

Much research into the development of high frequency fatigue testing and measuring equipment has been developed to-date. This includes a range of types such as forced-mechanical, resonant-electromagnetic as well as Piezo-electrical based testing machines. These are further discussed in [1] who also outlines some important principles, testing procedures and important findings related to VHCF regime crack formation. Three important aspects relating to specimen quality are defined viz… Geometry, Surface Finish and Microstructure.

Interesting observations relating to local crack behaviour have been made through the research work of Murakami [2]. A quantifiable maximum stress intensity factor for both interior and surface cracks based on inclusion sizes have been developed and presented in [1] according equations (1) & (2) which allows for the estimation of crack growth rates.:

\[\begin{equation} K_{max,int} = 0.5 \cdot \Delta \sigma \cdot \sqrt{\pi} \cdot area^\frac{1}{4} \tag{1} \end{equation}\]

\[\begin{equation} K_{max,surf} = 0.65 \cdot \Delta \sigma \cdot \sqrt{\pi} \cdot area^\frac{1}{4} \tag{2} \end{equation}\]

Investigations into the prediction of critical crack lengths based on threshold stress intesity factors of small non-propagating cracks within ductile materials have also been developed. It could be shown that the threshold stress intensity factor is not only determined by the local cyclic stress but also the actual number of cycles. Once a low-stress, low-cycle threshold stress intensity intensity factor has been determined, the determination of the critical void or corrosion pits sizes can be made, this is particularly useful in turbine blade applications.

Typical micrographic features such as broken inclusion particles and non-propagating crack initiation sites can also be observed through the use of a SEM. Examples shown in [1] are shown in Figures 1 & 2.

Surface and sub-surface microstructure also poses a considerable impact on crack (growth) behaviour. In order to examine microstructural features on and within specimen surfaces, the use of SEM with electron backscattered diffraction has proven invaluable. Comprehensive use of an SEM system allows for the determination of grain orientations and slip planes which assist further in assessing the various factors influencing fatigue crack initiation within the microstructure. Identification of the microstructural behaviour during cyclic loading enables further insight to the question of the traditionally used fatigue limit beyond 108 cycles. The identification of inhomogeneities in this regard is therefore paramount where cracks are initiated at a sub-surface level often leading to failure after the fatigue limit has been reached.

Frequency Effects - Measuring Techniques

For strain rate insensitive alloys and non-corrosive environments, the use of different fatigue systems usually does NOT lead to intrinsic frequency influence [1]. Where initial cyclic- softening followed by cyclic hardening is observed, the frequency effect is indeed evident due the the strain-sensitivity of the material.

Observations shown in [3] shows no significant frequency effects on both strain rate and threshold stress intensity when tests were in vacuum. However in ambient air and high stress intensities fatigue cracks were found to propagate between 5 and 50 times faster at low frequency tests (20Hz) compared to high frequency tests (20kHz-Ultrasonic). These effects were however also dependent on materials with dominant planar slip vs. homogeneous slip (within 7075-OA/UA material) characteristics. Ultimately planar slip materials had shown better crack growth properties where growth rates were not affected by stress intensity. Therefore perhaps some further attention to homogeneous slip materials must be made when considering frequency effects. Figure 3 displays some of the test data showing that strain rate influences are small for constant specimen thickness used.

LSP Topics

“The major driving force in laser shock peening at this time is the often dramatic increase in fatigue life and fatigue strength. The degree of improvement achievable with laser shock peening falls into two general categories. The most dramatic increases in fatigue strength are achievable in thin sections for through-thickness cracks propagating into the structure from a stress riser associated with an edge, be it a hole, notch, corner, inclusion or other feature. Substantial increases in fatigue strength are also achieved in thicker structures laser peened on a single surface in the area of a stress riser or stress concentration.” [4]

Laser Wavelength

The wavelength of the laser influences residual stresses imparted into the material. From [5] it is mentioned that Fabbro et al. had investigated the effects of wavelengths on laser dielectric breakdown threshold on copper; noticing that a decrease in wavelength results in a decrease in threshold and therefore also a reduction in peak pressure .

Laser Power Density

It is widely accepted that the surface/interface stress amplitude increases with increasing pressure induced by the laser and therefore an increase in surface residual stress level. A power density threshold is however reached which causes a decrease in surface residual stress as the density increases (while still increasing through depth). According to [5], for water confined ablation mode, laser intensities can reach a threshold value of 0.5GW/cm2.

Laser Pulse Duration

Short pulse durations generally achieves larger plasma peak pressures. Fabbro et al also investigated the influence of pulse duration on pressure pulse and peak pressures noting that in confined mode the pressure pulse duration can be sustained between 3 to 4 times longer than laser pulse durations and therefore peak pressures.

Laser Beam Spot Size and Geometry

Typical Much the same as the power density, the laser spot size also affects the residual stress profile. This is particularly noticable for surface residual stress levels. Larger spot sizes also produces compressive stress levels further beneath the treated surface. In some cases however the surface compressive residual stresses does not increase with increasing spot size but is usually highest at the surface.

It is mentioned in [6] that when circular spot geomteries are used, the residual stresses at the centre becomes unstable due to complex shock interactions. Modifying the spot geometry or changing coverage ratios may mitigate this phenomenon.

Life Extension of 7xxx Series Aluminium

Important characteristics in Laser Shock treatments, particularly on aluminium 7xxx series alloys are discussed in [6]. LSP shows significant advantages at high stress level and therefore presents an effective method for life extension. Critical conditions for internal cracking were also identified.

In order to enhance the fatigue life of metallic components an innovative approach using current laser technologies was designed. This involved utilising the energy within a laser beam in order to induce compressive residual stresses into surface and subsurface regions. Laser Shock Peening (LSP) was thus developed as a tool, particularly useful for aircraft/aerospace components, to enhance fatigue life relative to conventional shot peening.

The research conducted by [6] was designed to quantify fatigue life, within aircraft aluminium alloys, compared with Glass Bead Peening and identify possible risks therein. The report shows some of the following findings:

  • Where material and specimen geometry (configuration) allows, a 3-4 times increase in fatigue life could be achieved.
  • Fatigue life in LSP treated 7050 series aluminium specimens was REDUCED where laser power density was too high (>3GW/cm2), causes were not fully understood but were assumed to be the result of tensile residual stresses at the surface combined with compressive stresses at the sub-surface level caused by over-peening at the larger power level.
  • Internal crack initiation caused by LSP treatments also plays a significant role in the resulting fatigue life of the treated specimen.
  • Due to the high running costs of LSP treatments, benefits are currently only realised for high value components such as aircraft components which may require increased inspection intervals or reduced maintenance costs.
  • Of critical importance is a thorough understanding and measurement of the processing parameters involved as improper selection of processing parameters could also result in a shortening of the fatigue life.
  • Observation of ‘surface burning’ must be made after processing as this can be an initial indication that subsurface cracks could be present.

\(\color{green}{\textbf{Advantages of LSP treatment}}\)

  • Enhanced Fatigue Life (deeper compressive layer)
  • Improved Surface Finish Compared with Conventional Mechanical Peening (GLASS BEAD or STEEL SHOT)
  • Known to reduce fretting fatigue
  • Corrosion resistance enhancement
  • Increased resistance to foreign object damage


\(\color{red}{\textbf{Disdvantages of LSP treatment}}\)


  • Process parameters must be well understood and defined as poor paramter selection may result in fatigue life reduction caused by internal cracking
  • Complex surface geometries are difficult to treat effectively
